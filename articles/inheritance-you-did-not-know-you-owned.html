The Inheritance You Did Not Know You Owned

By Timothy S. Krause

4 min read

The Hidden Digital Estate

When loved ones pass away, families inventory physical possessions: homes, vehicles, jewelry, and bank accounts. But in 2026, a significant asset class often goes completely unnoticed: domain names. At DomanID, we've facilitated numerous domain inheritance transfers, uncovering valuable digital assets that families never knew existed. This guide reveals how domains become hidden inheritances and what you need to know about claiming, valuing, and managing these digital properties.

Why Domains Become Forgotten Assets

Several factors cause domains to slip through estate planning cracks:

Invisible Ownership

Unlike physical property, domains exist only as registry entries. There's no deed, no certificate, no physical manifestation. Unless explicitly documented, heirs may never discover their existence.

Auto-Renewal Obscurity

Domains renew automatically through credit cards on file. Families see charges on statements but may not recognize them as valuable assets worth investigating.

Registrar Communication Gaps

Registrars typically communicate only with account holders. When owners pass, notices go to email addresses the deceased controlled—emails families may not access.

Lack of Estate Documentation

Most wills and trusts don't specifically mention digital assets. Without explicit inclusion, domains may not be distributed according to owner intentions.

Discovering Inherited Domains

If you suspect a loved one owned domains:

Email Search

Search the deceased's email for registrar communications: GoDaddy, Namecheap, Google Domains, etc. Look for renewal notices, invoices, or account confirmations.

Credit Card Statements

Review statements for recurring charges from domain registrars. Even $15-20 annual charges can reveal valuable assets.

WHOIS Lookup

Search WHOIS databases using the deceased's name, email, or phone number. This reveals domains registered in their name.

Password Managers

Check password management tools for registrar login credentials. Many investors store these digitally for convenience.

Registrar Direct Contact

Contact major registrars with death certificates and estate documentation. They can identify accounts associated with the deceased.

Legal Transfer Process

Inheriting domains requires proper legal procedures:

Executor Authority

The estate executor must have legal authority to access and transfer digital assets. Ensure wills explicitly grant this power.

Documentation Requirements

  • Death certificate (certified copies)
  • Letters testamentary or court appointment documents
  • Valid government identification
  • Registrar-specific inheritance forms

Registrar Policies

Each registrar has different inheritance procedures. Some require extensive documentation; others have streamlined processes. Plan for 2-8 weeks for completion.

Account Access vs. Domain Transfer

You may gain account access without full ownership transfer. For long-term security, transfer domains to heir-controlled accounts.

Valuing Inherited Domains

Not all inherited domains are valuable. Assess systematically:

Extension Quality

.COM domains hold highest value. Alternative extensions (.NET, .ORG, .INFO) have lower liquidity and value.

Name Quality

  • Premium: Short, memorable, dictionary words, brandable names
  • Standard: Descriptive keyword combinations
  • Low Value: Long, hyphenated, obscure, or trend-based names

Development Status

Developed domains with traffic or revenue have verifiable value. Undeveloped domains require appraisal based on comparable sales.

Professional Appraisal

Tax Implications

Inherited domains carry specific tax considerations:

Step-Up in Basis

In many jurisdictions, inherited assets receive "step-up" in basis to fair market value at date of death. This can reduce capital gains taxes on future sales.

Estate Tax Thresholds

High-value domain portfolios may contribute to estate tax liability. Document valuations properly for tax filings.

Income Tax on Sales

When selling inherited domains, capital gains tax applies to appreciation since inheritance date (not original acquisition date).

Professional Guidance

Consult tax professionals familiar with digital asset inheritance. Proper documentation prevents future tax complications.

Decision Framework: Keep or Sell

After inheriting domains, evaluate strategically:

Reasons to Keep

  • Domains have significant appraised value
  • Family sentimental or historical significance
  • Ongoing revenue from parking or leasing
  • Strategic value for family businesses

Reasons to Sell

  • Renewal costs exceed potential value
  • No family interest in managing digital assets
  • Immediate liquidity needs for estate expenses
  • Domains are low-quality with minimal appreciation potential

Preventing Your Own Domains from Becoming Lost

Protect your heirs through proactive planning:

  • Document Everything: Maintain list of all domains, registrars, and account credentials
  • Update Wills: Explicitly include digital assets in estate planning documents
  • Designate Digital Executor: Appoint someone knowledgeable about digital assets
  • Share Access: Ensure trusted family members can access password managers and accounts
  • Regular Reviews: Update documentation annually as portfolio changes

Common Inheritance Mistakes

  • Letting Domains Expire: Failing to renew during transfer process loses assets permanently
  • Selling Too Quickly: Liquidating without proper valuation leaves money on table
  • Ignoring Renewal Costs: Inheriting hundreds of domains creates unexpected expenses
  • Poor Documentation: Inadequate records complicate future estate transfers
